# New Game Options — Learner Experience Guide

How each new game option is introduced to and experienced by the learner.
For each: what they see, what they do, what makes it fun, and how the math is explained.

---

## Montessori-Inspired Options

### 1. Golden Beads (golden-beads)
**Standards:** K.NBT.A.1, 1.NBT.B.2a-c, 2.NBT.A.1a-b

**What the learner sees:**
A workspace with loose golden beads (small circles) scattered on the left. On the right: an empty ten-frame (2×5 grid) and a hundred-square outline (10×10). The prompt says: "Show me 34 using beads!"

**What they do:**
- Drag individual beads into the ten-frame slots
- When 10 slots fill up, they auto-bundle into a ten-bar (a rod of 10 connected beads) with a satisfying snap animation
- Drag ten-bars into the hundred-square
- The display shows: "3 tens and 4 ones = 34"

**What makes it fun:**
- The bundling animation is satisfying (beads snap together like magnets)
- A counter shows how many bundles they've made
- The character cheers when a ten-bar forms
- Speed challenge: later rounds have bigger numbers (134 = 1 hundred + 3 tens + 4 ones)

**How the math is explained (intro screen):**
"Numbers are made of ones, tens, and hundreds. When you get 10 ones, they bundle into a ten. When you get 10 tens, they make a hundred! Drag beads to show the number."

### 2. Hundred Board (hundred-board)
**Standards:** K.CC.A.1, K.CC.A.2, K.CC.B.4c

**What the learner sees:**
A 10×10 empty grid. Numbers appear one at a time floating above the grid. Some cells already have numbers as guides (1, 10, 50, 100).

**What they do:**
- A number appears (e.g., "23")
- Player clicks the correct cell on the grid where 23 belongs (row 3, column 3)
- Correct placement: number drops into cell with a glow
- Wrong placement: cell flashes red, number bounces back

**What makes it fun:**
- Numbers come faster each round
- Patterns emerge as the board fills up (columns are same ones digit!)
- Bonus points for streaks
- Later rounds: some guide numbers are removed

**How the math is explained:**
"Every number has a home on the hundred board. The rows go by tens: 1-10, 11-20, 21-30... Find where each number lives!"

---

### 3. Stamp Game (stamp-game)
**Standards:** 2.NBT.A.3, 2.NBT.B.8, 2.NBT.B.9

**What the learner sees:**
Four colored tile stacks: green tiles labeled "1", blue tiles labeled "10", red tiles labeled "100", large green tiles labeled "1000". A target number at the top.

**What they do:**
- Click tiles to add them to the workspace
- Each click adds one tile and updates the running total by place value
- "345" = click red 3 times + blue 4 times + green 5 times
- Display shows: "3 hundreds + 4 tens + 5 ones = 345"
- Press "Check" when done

**What makes it fun:**
- Tiles stack up visually in columns by color
- Trading: click a "trade" button to exchange 10 ones for 1 ten (with animation)
- Later rounds: "Add 10 to 345" — player adds one blue tile
- Sound effects for each tile placed

**How the math is explained:**
"Stamps come in different sizes: 1s, 10s, 100s, and 1000s. Use the right stamps to build any number. Each place value has its own color!"

---

### 4. Fraction Circles (fraction-circles)
**Standards:** 4.NF.C.5, enhances all fraction standards

**What the learner sees:**
A whole circle on the left (the "pizza"). On the right, fraction pieces: halves, thirds, fourths, fifths, etc. The prompt shows a fraction: "Show 3/4"

**What they do:**
- Click on wedge-shaped pieces to select them
- Pieces snap into the circle
- The circle fills with colored wedges
- A fraction label updates as pieces are placed: "1/4... 2/4... 3/4 ✓"

**What makes it fun:**
- Pieces physically fit together like a pizza puzzle
- Can compare fractions: "Which is bigger, 3/4 or 2/3?" — fill two circles side by side
- Color-coded by denominator (fourths are blue, thirds are red)
- Equivalent fractions: show that 2/4 fills the same space as 1/2

**How the math is explained:**
"Fractions are parts of a whole. The bottom number (denominator) tells how many equal pieces. The top number (numerator) tells how many pieces to take. Fill the circle!"

---

### 5. Bead Chain (bead-chain)
**Standards:** 5.NBT.A.2, 5.NBT.A.3a, skip counting

**What the learner sees:**
A long horizontal chain of colored beads stretching across the screen. A counter at zero. The prompt: "Skip count by 7 — what's the 5th number?"

**What they do:**
- Click a bead to advance the count
- Each click highlights the next group of beads and shows the running number
- Click, click, click: 7, 14, 21, 28, 35 — the chain highlights groups
- Type the final answer

**What makes it fun:**
- Different chain colors for different skip counts (7s = white, like Montessori)
- The chain physically moves/scrolls as you count
- Speed mode: how fast can you skip count to the answer?
- Visual pattern: highlighted groups create a rhythm along the chain

**How the math is explained:**
"Bead chains help you see multiplication as skip counting. Counting by 7s: each click jumps 7 beads. After 5 jumps, you've counted 5 × 7 = 35!"

---

### 6. Checkerboard Multiply (checkerboard-multiply)
**Standards:** 4.NBT.B.6

**What the learner sees:**
A color-coded grid (green, blue, red columns for ones, tens, hundreds). One number across the top, another down the side. The prompt: "46 × 3 = ?"

**What they do:**
- Click grid cells to "place beads" at intersections
- Each cell shows the partial product (6×3=18, 40×3=120)
- Partial products are shown in colored columns matching place value
- Sum the partial products: 18 + 120 = 138

**What makes it fun:**
- Color coding makes place value visible (ones are green, tens are blue)
- Beads physically appear at intersections
- Later rounds: larger numbers with more columns
- The grid reveals the structure of multiplication

**How the math is explained:**
"The checkerboard breaks big multiplication into small pieces. 46 × 3 = (40 × 3) + (6 × 3) = 120 + 18 = 138. Each cell shows one piece!"

## Singapore/CPA-Inspired Options

### 7. Bar Model (bar-model)
**Standards:** 2.MD.A.3, word problem enhancement

**What the learner sees:**
A word problem at the top. An empty workspace below. A tray of colored rectangular bars of different lengths.

**What they do:**
- Read: "Sam has 24 apples. He gives 9 away. How many are left?"
- Drag a long bar for 24 (the whole)
- Drag a shorter bar for 9 (the part given away)
- The gap between them is highlighted: "? = 24 - 9"
- Type the answer: 15

**What makes it fun:**
- Bars physically snap and resize
- Word problems feel like puzzles, not worksheets
- Later rounds: two-step problems with multiple bars
- "Can you draw the model before solving?" — visual before symbolic

**How the math is explained:**
"Bar models turn word problems into pictures. The big bar is the total. The small bars are the parts. The missing piece is what you need to find!"

---

### 8. Place Value Discs (place-value-discs)
**Standards:** Place value, regrouping enhancement

**What the learner sees:**
A place value mat with columns: Ones, Tens, Hundreds. Colored discs (yellow=1, red=10, blue=100). A number to build or an addition problem to solve.

**What they do:**
- Drag discs into columns to build a number
- When 10 ones pile up, a "Trade" button glows
- Click Trade: 10 yellow discs animate into 1 red disc (regrouping!)
- For addition: build both numbers, then trade when columns overflow

**What makes it fun:**
- The trading animation is satisfying (10 small discs merge into 1 big one)
- Builds understanding of why "carrying" works
- Later rounds: subtraction with "borrowing" (break a ten into 10 ones)

**How the math is explained:**
"Place value discs show what numbers are made of. When you get 10 ones, trade them for 1 ten. When you get 10 tens, trade for 1 hundred. It's like exchanging coins!"

## Standard Pedagogy Options

### 9. Algebra Tiles (expression-transformer)
**Standards:** 6.EE.A.2a-b, 6.EE.A.3, 7.EE.A.1-2

**What the learner sees:**
A workspace with colored tiles: small green squares (= 1), small red squares (= -1), blue rectangles (= x), and large blue squares (= x²). An expression to build at the top.

**What they do:**
- "Build 3x + 5": drag 3 blue rectangles and 5 green squares
- "Simplify 2x + 3 + x + 2": combine tiles (3 blue + 5 green = 3x + 5)
- "Factor 6 + 3x": arrange tiles into a rectangle showing 3(2 + x)
- Zero pairs: drag a +1 and -1 together, they cancel with a poof animation

**What makes it fun:**
- Physical manipulation of abstract algebra
- Zero pairs disappearing is satisfying
- Factoring = arranging tiles into rectangles (like Tetris!)
- "Can you build this expression using fewer tiles?"

**How the math is explained:**
"Algebra tiles make expressions real. The blue rectangle is x (we don't know its value yet). Green squares are positive numbers, red squares are negative. Combine them to simplify!"

**Pedagogical backing:** Algebra tiles are a proven CRA manipulative used in middle school math worldwide. Research shows they significantly improve understanding of algebraic concepts (Hand2Mind research, NCTM recommendations).

### 10. Factor Finder (factor-finder)
**Standards:** 4.OA.B.4, 6.NS.B.4

**What the learner sees:**
A number at the top (e.g., "36"). An empty tree structure below. Factor "seed" numbers on the sides.

**What they do:**
- Start: 36 at the top
- Split: click to branch into 2 × 18, or 4 × 9, or 6 × 6
- Keep splitting until all leaves are prime numbers
- For GCF: build trees for TWO numbers, find common prime factors
- For LCM: multiply all factors together

**What makes it fun:**
- Tree grows as you factor — visual and organic
- Multiple valid paths (36 = 2×18 = 2×2×9 = 2×2×3×3)
- Racing: who can find all prime factors fastest?
- Satisfying when leaves turn green (all prime!)

**How the math is explained:**
"Every number can be broken into smaller numbers that multiply together. Keep breaking until you reach prime numbers — numbers that can't be split further. These are the building blocks!"

**Pedagogical backing:** Factor trees are a standard math pedagogy tool recommended by NCTM and used in virtually every middle school math curriculum.

### 11. Category Sort (category-sort)
**Standards:** K.MD.B.3

**What the learner sees:**
Objects scattered in the middle (animals, shapes, colors, sizes). Labeled bins at the bottom: "Red", "Blue", "Green" (or "Big", "Small", etc.)

**What they do:**
- Drag each object into the correct bin
- When all objects are sorted, count each bin
- "How many red objects?" → type the number
- Later rounds: sort by TWO attributes ("Big AND red")

**What makes it fun:**
- Objects are fun (animals, toys, food)
- Bins fill up with a stacking animation
- "Can you predict which bin will have the most?"
- Sorting IS classifying — the gameplay is the math

**How the math is explained:**
"Sort the objects into groups. Then count how many are in each group. This is how scientists organize data!"

---

### 12. Measure and Plot (measure-and-plot)
**Standards:** 2.MD.D.9, 3.MD.B.4, 4.MD.B.4, 5.MD.B.2

**What the learner sees:**
A ruler at the top. Objects to measure. A blank line plot (number line with x-axis) at the bottom.

**What they do:**
- Drag an object next to the ruler
- Read the measurement and type it
- The measurement appears as an X on the line plot
- After measuring all objects, the line plot is complete
- "What is the most common length?" → read from the plot

**What makes it fun:**
- Measuring real-ish objects (pencils, bugs, leaves)
- The line plot builds as you go — data visualization in real time
- Later rounds: measurements in fractions (2½ inches)
- "What's the difference between the longest and shortest?"

**How the math is explained:**
"Measure each object. Then plot your measurements on the number line. Each X marks one measurement. Where do the Xs stack up? That's the most common length!"

**Pedagogical backing:** This directly mirrors the Common Core standard's intent — generate measurement data and represent it on a line plot. It's the concrete → pictorial progression from CPA.

---

### 13. Inequality Grapher (inequality-grapher)
**Standards:** 6.EE.B.8

**What the learner sees:**
A real-world scenario: "The temperature must be above 32°F for the ice cream truck to come out." A number line below. Inequality symbols on the side.

**What they do:**
- Read the scenario
- Write the inequality: "t > 32"
- Drag an open or closed circle to 32 on the number line
- Drag an arrow to shade the correct direction (right for >, left for <)
- The number line highlights with color: "All these temperatures work!"

**What makes it fun:**
- Real-world scenarios (roller coaster heights, speed limits, temperatures)
- Shading is physical — drag to paint the number line
- Open vs closed circle: "Is 32 included or not?"
- Later rounds: "x ≤ 5 AND x > 2" (compound inequalities)

**How the math is explained:**
"Inequalities show a range of possible answers. Not just ONE number — MANY numbers work! The circle shows where the range starts. The arrow shows which direction. Shade all the numbers that make it true!"

**Pedagogical backing:** Research shows that graphing on a number line with open/closed circles and shading is the standard concrete representation for inequalities, recommended by NCTM.

---

### 14. Scientific Notation Explorer (sci-notation)
**Standards:** 8.EE.A.4

**What the learner sees:**
Two cards showing real-world quantities: "Distance from Earth to Mars: 225,000,000 km" and "Size of a red blood cell: 0.000007 m". A converter tool.

**What they do:**
- Slide a "power of 10" slider to convert the number
- Watch the decimal point move as the exponent changes
- Type the scientific notation: 2.25 × 10⁸
- Multiply/divide two sci-notation numbers by combining exponents

**What makes it fun:**
- Real space/biology/chemistry quantities (kids love extreme numbers)
- The decimal point physically slides left/right with the slider
- "How many blood cells fit between Earth and Mars?" — mind-blowing numbers
- Speed round: convert as fast as you can

**How the math is explained:**
"Scientists deal with HUGE and TINY numbers. Scientific notation makes them manageable: move the decimal point and count the moves. 225,000,000 = 2.25 × 10⁸ (moved 8 places left!)"

**Pedagogical backing:** The sliding decimal visualization is a standard teaching tool. The real-world context (astronomy, biology) is recommended by CCSS for making scientific notation relevant.
