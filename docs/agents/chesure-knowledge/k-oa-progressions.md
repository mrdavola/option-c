## K.OA — Operations and Algebraic Thinking

### Core progression at K level:
1. Students first learn to REPRESENT addition and subtraction with concrete objects
2. They model "put together / add to" and "take apart / take from" situations
3. They decompose numbers into pairs (e.g., 5 = 2 + 3, 5 = 4 + 1)
4. They find the number that makes 10 (foundational for later place value)
5. Symbolic notation (equations like 3 + 2 = 5) comes AFTER extensive concrete work

### Key pedagogical points:
- **Concrete before symbolic:** K students should NOT start with equations. They manipulate objects first, then the teacher shows the equation as a RECORDING of what happened.
- **Multiple representations:** Objects, fingers, drawings, verbal, then equations. The standard lists these in intentional order.
- **Problem types at K:** "Add to" (3 birds, 2 more come), "Take from" (5 birds, 2 fly away), "Put together" (3 red, 2 blue, how many total), "Take apart" (5 birds, how many are red/blue).
- **Numbers within 10:** K operations stay within 10. Within 5 for fluency.

### K.OA.A.1 specifically:
"Represent addition and subtraction with objects, fingers, mental images, drawings, sounds (e.g., claps), acting out situations, verbal explanations, expressions, or equations."

This is about REPRESENTATION, not calculation. The student shows the math using multiple forms. The emphasis is on the PROCESS of representing, not getting the answer quickly.

### K.OA.A.3 specifically:
"Decompose numbers less than or equal to 10 into pairs in more than one way."

Key: "more than one way" — students should discover that 5 = 1+4 AND 5 = 2+3 AND 5 = 3+2. The emphasis is on FLEXIBILITY of decomposition, not one right answer.

### K.OA.A.4 specifically:
"For any number from 1 to 9, find the number that when added to the given number makes 10."

This is the "making 10" strategy — foundational for later addition within 20 (e.g., 8+5 = 8+2+3 = 10+3 = 13).

### K.OA.A.5 specifically:
"Fluently add and subtract within 5."

"Fluently" at K means within a few seconds. Not instant recall, but efficient strategies (counting on, counting back, known facts).

## Common K-level misconceptions (to check for in games):

1. **Counting all vs counting on:** K students initially count ALL objects from 1 (1,2,3...4,5,6,7). More advanced: count ON from the larger number (start at 5, count 5,6,7). Games should support BOTH strategies.

2. **Confusing the operation with the objects:** A student might think "plus means more things appear" without understanding that addition means COMBINING two existing groups.

3. **Order irrelevance:** Young students may not yet understand that 3+2 = 2+3 (commutativity). Games should accept both orderings.

4. **Equation as recording vs instruction:** If a game shows "3 + 2 = ?" as a PROMPT, the student reads it as an instruction ("find 5"). If the game shows the equation AFTER the student combined objects, it's a RECORDING ("you just did 3 + 2 = 5"). The Progressions strongly prefer the latter at K level.

## Proven digital tools for K.OA (from Math Learning Center):

- **Number Frames:** Ten-frames where students place counters. Industry standard for K-2 addition/subtraction representation.
- **Number Rack (Rekenrek):** Rows of beads in groups of 5 and 10. Good for building number sense and seeing 5-structure.
- **Number Line:** For later K / early grade 1 — hop along a number line to add/subtract.

These are the ONLY digital tools we should reference. Do not invent new manipulatives.
